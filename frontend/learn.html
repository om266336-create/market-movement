Stock Market Basics

5 min read ‚Ä¢ Beginner

The stock market is a collection of exchanges where stocks (pieces of ownership in businesses) are bought and sold. It's one of the most vital components of a free-market economy.

How Does It Work?

Companies list shares of their stock on an exchange through a process called an Initial Public Offering (IPO). Investors can then buy and sell these shares among themselves, and the exchange tracks the supply and demand of each listed stock.

üí° Key Concept

Stock prices are determined by supply and demand. When more people want to buy a stock (demand) than sell it (supply), the price goes up. The opposite is also true.

Major Stock Exchanges

  • NYSE (New York Stock Exchange) - The largest stock exchange in the world by market capitalization
  • NASDAQ - Known for technology stocks like Apple, Microsoft, and Google
  • LSE (London Stock Exchange) - One of the oldest exchanges in the world

Market Hours

The US stock market is open Monday through Friday from 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM Eastern Time. There's also pre-market trading (4:00-9:30 AM) and after-hours trading (4:00-8:00 PM).

Understanding Stocks

7 min read ‚Ä¢ Beginner

A stock represents partial ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you're buying a small piece of that company, called a "share."

Types of Stocks

üîµ Common Stock

Most typical form of stock. Gives voting rights and potential dividends, but you're last in line for assets if the company goes bankrupt.

üü£ Preferred Stock

Usually no voting rights, but you get dividends before common stockholders and have higher claim on assets.

üü¢ Growth Stocks

Companies expected to grow faster than average. They typically reinvest profits rather than paying dividends.

üü° Value Stocks

Stocks trading below their intrinsic value. Often pay dividends and are considered undervalued by the market.

EPS Earnings Per Share - company's profit divided by shares outstanding

Reading Stock Charts

8 min read ‚Ä¢ Intermediate

Stock charts visually represent price movements over time. Understanding how to read them is essential for making informed investment decisions.

Chart Types

üìà Line Chart

Simplest form - connects closing prices over time. Good for seeing overall trends.

üìä Bar Chart

Shows open, high, low, and close (OHLC) for each period. More detailed than line charts.

üïØÔ∏è Candlestick

Most popular among traders. Shows OHLC with color-coded bodies (green=up, red=down).

Key Chart Patterns

üìà Uptrend Higher highs and higher lows - bullish signal
üìâ Downtrend Lower highs and lower lows - bearish signal
‚û°Ô∏è Support Price level where buying pressure prevents further decline

Technical Indicators

10 min read ‚Ä¢ Intermediate

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations based on price, volume, or open interest. They help predict future price movements.

Popular Indicators

üìä RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Measures the speed and magnitude of price movements on a scale of 0-100.

  • Above 70: Overbought - stock may be overvalued
  • Below 30: Oversold - stock may be undervalued
  • 50 area: Neutral territory

üìà MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

Shows the relationship between two moving averages of a stock's price.

  • MACD crosses above signal line: Bullish signal
  • MACD crosses below signal line: Bearish signal

üìâ Moving Averages (MA)

Smooths out price data to identify trends.

  • MA 20: Short-term trend (20 days)
  • MA 50: Medium-term trend (50 days)
  • MA 200: Long-term trend (200 days)
  • Golden Cross: 50-day MA crosses above 200-day (bullish)
  • Death Cross: 50-day MA crosses below 200-day (bearish)

Risk Management

7 min read ‚Ä¢ Essential

Risk management is crucial for long-term investing success. It's not just about making profits, but protecting your capital.

‚ö†Ô∏è Golden Rule

Never invest money you can't afford to lose. The stock market can be volatile, and past performance doesn't guarantee future results.

Key Risk Management Strategies

üéØ Diversification

Don't put all eggs in one basket. Spread investments across different sectors, asset classes, and geographies.

üìè Position Sizing

Never risk more than 1-2% of your portfolio on a single trade. This limits damage from any single loss.

üõë Stop Losses

Set predetermined exit points to limit losses. A 10-20% stop loss is common for swing trading.

üìä Risk/Reward Ratio

Only take trades where potential reward is at least 2-3x the potential risk.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • FOMO Trading - Buying because everyone else is
  • Averaging Down - Adding to losing positions
  • Overtrading - Trading too frequently
  • Ignoring Fundamentals - Only looking at charts
  • No Exit Strategy - Not knowing when to sell
